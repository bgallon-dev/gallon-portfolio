Design Philosophy

My work sits at the boundary between archival preservation, computational systems, and the ethics of interpretation.

  • Determinism over opacity: Systems should produce reproducible outputs. Verification workflows must be inspectable and versioned.
  • Auditability as baseline: Archival and research infrastructure should preserve process metadata, decision logs, and verifiable state transitions.
  • Structure before retrieval: Historical and archival materials require schema constraints and meaningful segmentation before search or model interaction.
  • Interpretation under evidentiary constraint: Scholarly and public-history outputs remain traceable to structured sources and documented transformations.

Challenging History Through Ritual: The IWW, Civic Liturgy, and Historiographical Forgetting in Spokane

Impact

  • Presented peer-reviewed research at Northwest Historians Guild.
  • Reframed the Spokane Free Speech Fight through ritual analysis.
  • Produced conference-ready materials and interpretive frameworks for public engagement.

Conference abstract. Problem: Mainstream histories of the 1909-10 Spokane Free Speech Fight often flatten the IWW campaign into strategy and spectacle, which obscures its moral and symbolic structure. What I produced: A conference presentation that interprets public arrests, constitutional language, and repeated suffering as civic ritual, with focused analysis of how Elizabeth Gurley Flynn is frequently misread. What changed: The project offers a counter-historiographical frame that restores ritual meaning to the event and gives public-history audiences a clearer interpretive model.

Tags: labor history, IWW, Spokane, civic ritual, historiography

Full abstract (program text)

This presentation interrogates the “ritual forgetting” that shapes mainstream histories of the 1909-10 Spokane Free Speech Fight, focusing on how the archival memory of the Industrial Workers of the World (IWW) has been systematically flattened by the very discipline entrusted to remember it. Instead of viewing the Wobblies’ campaign as mere labor agitation, this chapter reads their public suffering, constitutional language, and repeated arrests as a sacramental civic ritual designed to expose moral fractures at the heart of American democracy. Yet, as I show, canonical labor historians from Paul Brissenden to Melvyn Dubofsky have routinely interpreted these actions through paradigms of strategy and spectacle, denying the movement’s theological and symbolic grammar. Drawing on Victor Turner’s ritual theory and Carl Jung’s concept of the shadow, I argue that this misrecognition functions as a ritualized scholarly projection that sanitizes dissent and reproduces exclusion. Special attention is given to the gendered misreading of figures like Elizabeth Gurley Flynn, whose suffering is often sentimentalized rather than understood as liturgical defiance. In challenging these historiographical conventions, my work proposes a “counter-historiography,” one that restores the sacramental structure of protest to the center of Pacific Northwest labor memory, reframing the historian’s task as a moral act of excavation and symbolic recovery. This approach not only revises our understanding of the Free Speech Fight but calls into question the very rituals by which collective memory is shaped and policed in the Northwest and beyond.

Bio (program text): Benjamin Gallon is a history major at Eastern Washington University and Vice President of the university’s Phi Alpha Theta chapter. His research centers on labor activism and civic ritual in the Pacific Northwest, with a particular focus on the Industrial Workers of the World and the Spokane Free Speech Fight. Gallon’s work synthesizes ritual theory, depth psychology, and regional historiography to reinterpret protest as public liturgy. He is committed to challenging conventional narratives and restoring ethical meaning to marginalized histories in the region.

When Speech Learns Silence: Silence, Alterity, and the Ethics of Understanding in Villoro’s Philosophy

Methodological essay. Manuscript under peer review.

Public summary: This essay argues that efforts to make experience, history, and other cultures intelligible carry a structural risk: translation and coherence tend toward assimilation and, in political form, domination. Through a close reading of Luis Villoro’s work on mysticism, language, and power, I reconstruct a procedural ethic in which silence functions inside discourse as disciplined restraint rather than expressive failure. The paper then extends this framework to intercultural philosophy and historiography, proposing that scholarly and civic practices can preserve plurality by citing, translating, and deliberating without absorbing.

Tags: ethics of interpretation, silence, alterity, domination, historiography

Why it matters (for historians & archives)
  • Historiography: Coherence can become a form of ownership; ethical interpretation preserves gaps, limits, and unassimilated difference.
  • Archives / public history: Description should not overwrite source worlds; restraint can be an institutional practice (how we cite, translate, and frame).
  • Systems thinking: Integrity is not only verification; it is also a limit on what our tools and narratives are entitled to claim.
